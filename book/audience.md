# Book: Audience

## Primary reader
The mechanically capable Jeep XJ owner who does their own maintenance and repairs. They have replaced brakes, done an oil change, maybe swapped a starter or an alternator. They are comfortable with basic hand tools and are not afraid to read a wiring diagram — with some guidance.

## What they know
- How to use a socket set, torque wrench, and DVOM
- Basic automotive electrical: 12V, ground, fuse, relay
- How their Jeep works mechanically
- That their ECU is old and may be causing problems

## What they do not know (assume nothing)
- What a pull-up resistor is
- What VR vs Hall effect means
- What VE table, AFR, MAP, or STFT stand for
- How engine management software works
- Anything about open source ECU projects

## What they care about
- **Reliability** — especially on the trail, far from help
- **Understanding their own vehicle** — not just following steps blindly
- **Cost** — they are doing this themselves to save money and learn
- **The satisfaction of doing it right**

## What they do not care about
- Peak dyno numbers
- Racing applications
- Emissions compliance (mostly — note exceptions)
- Impressing other enthusiasts with complexity

## Writing implications
- Define every technical term on first use — no exceptions
- Explain the why before the how — every procedure, every time
- Never condescend about mechanical tasks the reader already knows
- Never assume the reader knows which era their XJ is without showing them how to confirm it
- Trail reliability is a first-class design concern — call it out whenever a choice affects it
- A reader should be able to follow this book alone, in their garage, with no prior ECU experience
