# Writing: Safety Guidelines

## Core rule
Safety warnings appear before the step they apply to — never after. A reader who has already done the dangerous thing cannot be warned retroactively.

## What always requires a warning

### Electrical work
- Battery disconnect required before: removing ECU connectors, splicing harness wires, installing new relays, any work inside the fuse box
- Live circuit work (intentional): warn that the circuit is live, state the voltage, state the consequence of contact

### Fuel system
- Fuel pressure must be relieved before: disconnecting fuel lines, removing injectors, opening the fuel rail
- No open flames or sparks near fuel system work — state this explicitly every time

### Hot components
- Allow engine to cool before: removing sensors from hot metal, working near the exhaust manifold, handling coolant hoses

### Rotating parts
- Engine must not be cranked or running during: harness work near the serpentine belt, any work near the cooling fan

## What does NOT need a warning
- Standard hand tool use
- Removing bolts from cold, unpressurized, unelectrified components
- Visual inspection tasks

## Framing safety warnings
Be direct and specific — not generic.

**Too generic:** "Use caution when working with electrical systems."

**Correct:** "⚠️ WARNING: Fuel pressure remains in the rail after the engine is off. Before disconnecting any injector or fuel line, relieve pressure by removing the fuel pump fuse, cranking the engine until it stalls, and cranking once more to clear residual pressure."

## Injury vs damage
Distinguish between warnings about personal injury and warnings about vehicle damage. Both matter — but injury warnings are higher priority and should be stated first when both apply.
