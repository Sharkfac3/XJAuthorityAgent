# XJ Cherokee — Body Styles and Platform Overview

## Body styles

The XJ Cherokee was produced from 1984 through 2001 in two body configurations:

| Body | Common name | Door count including rear hatch |
|------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| 2-door | 2-door Cherokee | 3 doors (2 side + rear hatch) |
| 4-door | 4-door Cherokee | 5 doors (4 side + rear hatch) |

Both styles share the same 101.4" wheelbase. The 4-door has a longer rear body section — not a longer wheelbase. Both body styles were available at the 1984 model-year launch and remained in production through the final 2001 model year.

The 2-door was the original design. The 4-door was added before production launched and has always been the higher-volume seller.

---

## Wheelbase

**101.4" — both body styles.**

This is one of the smallest wheelbases ever used on a 4WD vehicle with full-size doors. It is the primary reason the XJ has exceptional approach/departure angles relative to its footprint.

## Unibody construction

The XJ has no separate frame. The body structure is the frame — floors, rockers, pillars, and firewall all carry structural load.

This distinguishes the XJ from body-on-frame vehicles (ZJ/WJ Grand Cherokee, TJ Wrangler, full-size pickups) in several ways that affect repair and modification decisions:

**Rust**
- Rust in the unibody structure is structural damage, not cosmetic damage. Rusted rockers, floors, or unibody rails compromise crash safety and can affect alignment geometry.
- See [`../systems/body/rust-locations.md`](../systems/body/rust-locations.md) and [`../systems/body/rust-inspection.md`](../systems/body/rust-inspection.md) for the high-failure locations.

**Lift**
- Suspension lift on a unibody changes geometry at the control arms, driveshafts, and CV angles rather than simply relocating the body. Lifts over 4.5" require extended control arms and additional geometry correction. The stock unibody rails are not designed to absorb body-mount impact loads the way a frame is.
- Common maximum for a well-executed street/trail build: 4–4.5" lift with extended arms.

**Collision repair**
- Structural collision damage requires welding or sectioning the unibody rather than unbolting a body panel from a frame. Frame rails are not separate components that can be swapped.

## MJ Comanche (related platform, out of scope)

The Jeep MJ Comanche (1986–1992) is a pickup truck built on the XJ platform. It shares the 4.0L engine family, AW4 transmission, axles, transfer cases, and much of the electrical architecture with the XJ Cherokee.

**Components that interchange freely:** engine, transmission, transfer case, axle assemblies, most electrical/ECU hardware.

**What does not interchange:** cab/bed structure, doors, unibody floor sections, firewall geometry.

The MJ is out of scope for this knowledgebase. Facts documented here about engines, transmissions, and axles generally apply to the Comanche, but no verification is performed against MJ-specific configurations.
