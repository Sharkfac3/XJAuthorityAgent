# XJ Rust Inspection

## Purpose
How to inspect an XJ for rust — whether assessing a prospective purchase or evaluating your own vehicle before a lift or repair.

For what to look for at each location, see [`rust-locations.md`](rust-locations.md). This file covers the inspection process: tools, sequence, and how to read what you find.

---

## Tools

- **Flashlight** — mandatory; critical rust hides in shadows under the vehicle and inside the cab
- **Screwdriver** — flat-blade, for the poke test; the single most useful diagnostic tool for rust assessment
- **Mirror** (optional) — helps see the top of the rear frame rails and the underside of floor pans without lying flat
- **Gloves** — the underside of a rusty XJ will cut unprotected hands

The poke test: press a screwdriver tip firmly against metal you suspect is corroded. Healthy steel will not yield. Rust-through means the screwdriver will penetrate or the metal will crumble. This is the definitive test — visual assessment alone misses rust that is progressing behind a thin surface skin.

---

## Understanding what you're looking at

| Condition | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Surface rust | Red-orange discoloration, no pitting, wipes off or sands off | Normal on a 25–40 year old vehicle; monitor |
| Scale rust | Flaking layers, visible pitting underneath | Progressing; address before it gets structural |
| Rust-through | Holes visible, metal crumbles, screwdriver penetrates | Structural repair required |
| Soft spot | Metal flexes or depresses under thumb pressure with no hole yet | Rust-through from the inside; repair soon |

---

## Inspection sequence

Work in this order. The rear frame rails are the highest-priority location; if they are gone, the rest of the assessment may not matter.

### Step 1: Rear frame rails and shackle mounts

Get under the rear of the vehicle. Find the shackle hanger — the U-shaped bracket where the rear leaf spring shackle pivots, located just ahead of the rear bumper near the rear wheel. The rear frame rail is the box section leading forward from this point.

- Poke the box section with a screwdriver in multiple spots
- Rock the shackle hanger by hand; it should feel solid
- Look up at the cargo floor from underneath with a flashlight

If the rear frame rails are gone or the shackle hanger is loose: **stop the assessment here.** This is a major structural repair. Evaluate whether the repair cost is justified before continuing.

### Step 2: Rocker panels

Walk the full length of the vehicle along each sill. The rocker runs from the front wheel opening to the rear wheel opening.

- Press firmly with your thumb every 6–8 inches along the full length
- Poke any soft, bubbled, or suspicious areas with a screwdriver
- Open the doors and press along the inner sill (the step area) as well

From inside the cab, lift the edge of the carpet where it meets the sill on both sides. Look and feel for rust at the floor/sill junction.

### Step 3: Floor pans

From inside the cab, pull back the carpet on the driver's side and in the rear passenger area. If the carpet is glued down or you can't remove it, press on it — a floor pan with holes or heavy rust will flex noticeably underfoot.

- Look for rust staining, soft areas, or holes
- Pay attention to the area directly behind and beside the transmission tunnel (above the exhaust)
- A musty or moldy smell when the carpet is disturbed indicates past or current water intrusion

### Step 4: Firewall

From the engine bay, look at the lower passenger-side firewall near the HVAC box. From inside, check the passenger footwell at the firewall base.

- Surface rust is common here; rust-through or large holes indicate chronic water intrusion
- If the firewall is badly rusted, assume the floor pans have been wet for years

### Step 5: Visual sweep

Complete a visual walk-around for:
- Lower quarter panels and rear lower corners (rust-through indicates moisture history)
- Door bottoms (rust indicates clogged drains and standing water inside doors)
- Spare tire carrier attachment points if an external carrier is fitted

## Red flags — conditions that may make an XJ not worth repairing

These are not absolute rules, but each represents a major repair cost and should be weighed against the vehicle's value:

- **Rear frame rails rusted through or shackle hangers loose** — requires cutting and welding new steel; structural repair
- **Both rockers rusted through along significant length** — major structural repair; rocker replacement is extensive labor
- **Floor pans rusted through in multiple areas with compromised adjacent rockers** — floor pan repair alone is manageable; combined with rockers it compounds significantly
- **Firewall rot combined with floor pan holes** — indicates the vehicle has been wet for a very long time; rust likely extends further than visible

A vehicle with surface rust and minor scale at several locations is a normal XJ. A vehicle with rust-through at the rear rails, both rockers, and multiple floor pan holes is typically not a cost-effective repair candidate unless the buyer has access to a welder and low-cost labor.
