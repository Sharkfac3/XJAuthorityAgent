# XJ Rust Locations

## Purpose
Document where XJs rust, ordered by structural severity. The XJ is a unibody — rust is not cosmetic damage on this vehicle; it is structural degradation. See [`overview.md`](overview.md) for why unibody construction changes the stakes.

---

## 1. Rear Frame Rails and Shackle Mounts

**Structural severity: Critical**

The rear frame rails are box-section members that run under the cargo floor from behind the rear seat to the rear bumper area, terminating at the shackle hangers where the rear leaf springs attach.

This is the most critical rust location on the XJ. The rear unibody rails are the anchor point for the rear suspension. If they are heavily corroded:
- The vehicle cannot be safely lifted at the rear jack points
- Shackle hanger failure under load is possible
- Replacement requires cutting out the rotted section and welding in new steel (repair kits from Rusty's Offroad, RuffStuff, and others are available)

**How to inspect:** Reach under the vehicle just ahead of the rear wheel. Find the shackle hanger — the U-shaped mount where the rear spring shackle pivots. Poke the surrounding box section with a screwdriver. Soft or penetrating = the metal is gone. Also inspect the floor of the rail from inside the cargo area if the carpet is pulled back.

**What to look for:** Surface rust on the outside is normal. Concern starts when the rail has visible holes, when the screwdriver sinks in, or when the shackle hanger mount feels loose or moves.

## 2. Rocker Panels

**Structural severity: High**

The rocker panels (also called sills) are the full-length structural members running the bottom of the body from the front wheel opening to the rear wheel opening. On a unibody vehicle, the rockers are load-bearing structure — not decorative trim.

The A-pillar and B-pillar both tie into the rocker at the base. The B-pillar carries the seatbelt anchor. Serious rocker rust-through compromises pillar attachment and reduces the vehicle's ability to protect occupants in a side impact.

**How to inspect:** Press firmly with your thumb along the full length of the rocker, inside and out. Soft spots or flex indicate rust-through. Use a screwdriver to poke any suspicious areas. Check inside the cab at the floor edge where the carpet meets the sill — rust here usually means the outer rocker is worse.

**What to look for:**
- Surface rust and flaking paint: normal, address eventually
- Scale or blistering: rust progressing, inspect soon
- Flex or soft spots when pressed: rust-through, structural repair needed
- Holes: structural repair required before safe use

## 3. Floor Pans

**Structural severity: Moderate to High**

The floor pans are common rust locations, especially:
- Driver's floor under the carpet, particularly near the firewall base and door sill
- Rear passenger floor area
- Transmission tunnel area above the exhaust/catalytic converter (heat + moisture)

Water enters primarily from the windshield seal, firewall grommets, and door seals (see [`water-intrusion.md`](water-intrusion.md)). It pools under the carpet, invisible until significant damage has occurred.

**How to inspect:** Pull back or remove the carpet. A damp or moldy smell when the carpet is disturbed is a warning sign. Look for rust staining, soft metal, or visible holes. Poke suspicious areas.

**Relationship to rockers:** Holes in the floor pan are a leading indicator that the adjacent rocker is in worse condition. The floor pan rusts from the top down; the rocker rusts from trapped moisture inside. They often fail together.

---

## 4. Spare Tire Carrier Mount

**Structural severity: Moderate**

XJs with an external spare tire mount (rear swing-out or bolt-on carrier) put load on the rear unibody at the carrier attachment points. This area is prone to rust and the mount can pull free of the body entirely if the surrounding metal is sufficiently corroded.

**Note:** Verification of spare tire mount rust as a discrete common failure mode is based on community observation. Tag as needs verification if sourcing for a buyer's guide.

**How to inspect:** Rock the spare tire carrier by hand. Any movement beyond the hinge pivot indicates the mount attachment points are compromised. Inspect the rear body panel around each mounting hole.

---

## 5. Lower Quarters and Rear Corners

**Structural severity: Low (cosmetic)**

The lower quarter panels and the rear lower corners behind the rear wheels are common areas for surface rust and rust-through. These are not structural members. They are indicators of the vehicle's moisture history — a heavily rusted quarter likely means water has been sitting somewhere for a long time.

**Why it matters in assessment:** A vehicle with severe cosmetic rust at the quarters almost certainly has worse rust hidden elsewhere. Use it as a screening indicator, not an isolated concern.

---

## 6. Firewall Lower Section

**Structural severity: Low to Moderate**

The lower firewall, particularly near the heater box drain and HVAC pass-throughs, is a known rust location. Water enters from a failed windshield seal or deteriorated firewall grommets (see [`water-intrusion.md`](water-intrusion.md)) and collects at the base of the firewall.

Firewall rust is not itself a structural failure mode in most cases, but it indicates chronic water intrusion — which means the floor pans and rockers have likely been wet for years.

**How to inspect:** From the engine bay, look at the lower firewall on the passenger side near the HVAC box. From inside, check the passenger footwell at the firewall base.

---

## 7. Door Bottoms

**Structural severity: None (cosmetic)**

Door drain holes at the bottom of each door cavity clog with debris over time. Water then pools inside the door and causes rust through the bottom of the door skin. This is a cosmetic issue — doors are not structural on a unibody — but it indicates that the vehicle has had standing water in the doors, which often means the door seals are also degraded and may be contributing to floor pan rust.
