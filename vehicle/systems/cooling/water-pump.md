# Water Pump

**Branch router:** [index.md](index.md)

## Purpose
Document the 4.0L water pump — construction, failure modes, inspection method, and replacement criteria.

## Scope
Belt-driven water pump on the 4.0L inline-six, all years 1988–2001. The pump design is consistent across the engine's run; failure modes and inspection procedure are the same for all years.

---

## Construction

The 4.0L water pump is:
- **Belt-driven** off the serpentine accessory belt
- **Impeller-style** — a spinning impeller draws coolant from the lower radiator hose and forces it through the engine block and head
- Mounted to the front of the engine block; the fan clutch and mechanical fan mount to the pump's nose shaft
- The impeller material varies by production year and supplier (cast iron or stamped steel), but failure modes are consistent regardless of impeller material

---

## Failure modes

### 1. Weep hole drip — bearing or seal failure (most common)
The water pump has a **weep hole** drilled through the pump body, located on the underside of the pump below the shaft. This hole is intentional. Its purpose is to vent coolant to the outside when the internal shaft seal begins to fail, providing early warning before the bearing deteriorates further or coolant enters the oil.

A small amount of residue at the weep hole is not unusual on a pump with some age. **Active dripping or coolant staining below the weep hole means the pump needs replacement.** The bearing is not serviceable; the pump is replaced as a unit.

The weep hole is located on the bottom of the pump body, forward of the block, roughly centered between the mounting flange and the pulley. Coolant staining will run down from this point.

### 2. Impeller erosion or cavitation
Running the cooling system with distilled water only (no antifreeze) for extended periods will corrode or erode the impeller over time. Antifreeze contains corrosion inhibitors that protect metal surfaces inside the cooling circuit. A pump with a corroded or eroded impeller moves less coolant at any given RPM and can contribute to overheating.

This failure mode typically has no external symptoms until overheating occurs. It is found on teardown.

### 3. Shaft wobble or pulley play
A pump with a worn or damaged bearing will develop play in the shaft. This is detectable by gripping the fan or pulley and checking for up-down or side-to-side movement. Any perceptible wobble is a sign of bearing failure — replace the pump before the bearing seizes or the fan departs.

## Inspection

Check the water pump at every major service interval or any time coolant work is performed:

1. **Weep hole check:** With the engine cold and off, look under the pump body for coolant staining or residue below the weep hole. A flashlight helps. Fresh coolant staining = replace.
2. **Pulley play check:** Grip the pulley or fan hub firmly and check for movement perpendicular to the shaft axis. There should be none. Any wobble = replace.
3. **Belt tension:** A loose serpentine belt reduces pump speed and cooling effectiveness. Confirm belt tension is within spec before blaming the pump for low flow.
4. **Noise:** A failing pump bearing can produce a growling, rumbling, or squealing noise that changes with engine RPM. Hard to isolate from other accessory belt noise, but persistent RPM-dependent noise from the front of the engine warrants a weep hole and wobble check.

---

## Replacement

### When to replace
- **Any active drip or staining at the weep hole**
- **Any detectable pulley wobble**
- Whenever the engine is apart for head work or major cooling service (labor cost savings make replacement practical)

There is no hard mileage interval supported by community data. Pump life varies widely — some pumps last the life of the vehicle, some fail at 60,000 miles. Condition-based inspection is more reliable than mileage-based replacement.

### What to replace at the same time
Since the pump drives fan mounting and is central to the cooling circuit, it is efficient to replace the following at the same service:
- Thermostat and housing gasket
- Serpentine belt (if not recently replaced)
- Any hose showing softness, swelling, or cracking
