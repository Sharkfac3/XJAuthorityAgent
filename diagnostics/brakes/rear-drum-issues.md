# Rear Drum Issues

## Purpose
Fault-isolate common XJ rear drum brake problems: seized self-adjusters, wheel cylinder leaks, and glazed or contaminated brake shoes.

## Symptom definition
Use this leaf when the rear brakes appear to be contributing to any of the following:
- Long or high brake pedal travel before braking effect begins
- Vehicle pulls to one side under braking (rear bias)
- Brake fluid visible or wet inside the drum area
- Soft or spongy pedal that persisted after the fronts were inspected and found serviceable
- Grinding or scraping from the rear under light brake application

## Do not use this leaf when
- The pedal sinks to the floor with firm sustained pressure and the rears are not the suspect → master cylinder bypass; see [`pedal-feel.md`](pedal-feel.md)
- The main complaint is vibration or pulsation → a rear drum that is out-of-round; requires drum machining or replacement

---

## Background: XJ rear drum configuration

All XJ Cherokees used front disc / rear drum brakes. The rear drums use a self-adjusting mechanism that operates during reverse braking: a star wheel adjuster is ratcheted by a cable as the shoes wear, maintaining proper shoe-to-drum clearance. When this mechanism seizes, the shoes remain at their last adjusted position and wear does not get compensated.

---

## Common branches

### Seized self-adjuster branch

**Symptom:** Long pedal travel; the pedal goes significantly farther before braking effect than it should. Pedal hydraulics may feel normal (firm, no sinking), but travel is excessive.

**Cause:** The star wheel adjuster corrodes over time and stops rotating. Without adjustment, as the shoes wear down, shoe-to-drum clearance increases. More fluid travel is required to move the shoes into contact.

**Visual inspection:**
1. Remove the drum (drum pulls straight off after removing the wheel; may require backing off the adjuster if the drum is reluctant)
2. Locate the star wheel adjuster — it sits between the two brake shoes toward the bottom of the assembly
3. Attempt to rotate the star wheel by hand or with an adjuster spoon
4. A seized adjuster will not rotate, or rotates with significant resistance and immediately re-seizes

**Manual adjustment procedure:** Rotate the star wheel to expand the adjuster until slight drag is felt on the drum, then back off until the drag disappears. This sets correct clearance temporarily. If the self-adjuster mechanism is seized, the adjustment will not hold — the adjuster assembly should be replaced.

**Self-adjuster operation check:** The auto-adjuster requires several firm forward stops followed by firm reverse stops to cycle correctly. If the adjuster cable or lever is bent, corroded, or incorrectly seated, automatic adjustment will not function even if the star wheel turns freely.

---

### Wheel cylinder leak branch

**Symptom:** Brake fluid visible inside the drum, wet/oily brake shoes, soft or spongy pedal after fronts have been ruled out.

**Cause:** The wheel cylinder contains two pistons that push outward against each shoe when hydraulic pressure is applied. The rubber piston seals deteriorate with age. A leaking wheel cylinder passes fluid past the seals, contaminating the shoes and losing hydraulic pressure from the circuit.

**Visual inspection:**
1. Remove the drum
2. Inspect the wheel cylinder (mounted at the top center of the brake assembly) for visible wetness, oily residue, or fluid weeping from the dust boot ends
3. Peel back each rubber dust boot slightly and check for fluid behind it — any fluid present indicates the seal is leaking

**Inspection note:** Brake fluid contaminates brake shoe friction material on contact. Shoes that have absorbed brake fluid cannot be dried out and reused. Replace shoes and wheel cylinder together; do not replace the cylinder and reuse contaminated shoes.

**Replace in pairs:** Wheel cylinders should be replaced on both sides even if only one is visibly leaking. The seals on both cylinders are the same age.

After replacement, bleed the rear brake circuit.

---

### Glazed or contaminated shoe branch

**Symptom:** Rear braking is weak or inconsistent; may squeal or grab intermittently; drum interior shows shiny hard spots or oily residue.

**Glazed shoes:** Sustained light drag or overheating bakes the friction material surface, reducing friction. The drum face also glazes. Lightly glazed surfaces can sometimes be scuffed with sandpaper; heavily glazed surfaces require replacement.

**Oil-contaminated shoes:** If a rear axle seal is leaking, gear oil will enter the drum and saturate the shoe material. This is a separate source of contamination from wheel cylinder leaks.
- Inspect the axle flange area for oil residue while the drum is off
- A leaking axle seal must be repaired before fitting new shoes or the new shoes will be contaminated immediately

---

## Exit criteria

- **Seized adjuster:** replace adjuster assembly; set manual clearance; confirm auto-adjust mechanism cycles correctly
- **Wheel cylinder leak:** replace both wheel cylinders; replace shoes if contaminated; bleed rears
- **Glazed/contaminated shoes:** identify and fix the contamination source before replacing shoes; replace drum if heavily glazed
