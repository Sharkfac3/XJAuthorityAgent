# Rear Mounting and Brackets — Sterling 10.50

Physical mounting, shock placement, traction control, and bump stop configuration for a 2005+ Super Duty Sterling 10.50 rear axle under a Jeep Cherokee XJ.

---

## Spring-over-axle (SOA)

Like the front, one-ton XJ rear builds run spring-over-axle. The leaf spring sits on top of the axle tube, with u-bolts clamping the assembly together.

### Spring-to-axle connection
- Leaf spring sits on the weld-on spring perch (see [`spring-perch-welding.md`](spring-perch-welding.md))
- U-bolts wrap over the spring and under the axle tube
- U-bolt plate sits under the tube — serves as the clamping surface and may incorporate lower shock mount tabs
- **U-bolt sizing:** size for the Sterling's 3.5" tube OD. Stock XJ u-bolts are too small.

### Spring selection
- Stock XJ rear leaf springs may be too soft for the added weight of the Sterling 10.50 (~130 lb heavier than stock rear)
- Options: add-a-leaf to the stock pack, aftermarket heavy-duty leaf springs, or custom spring packs rated for the total vehicle weight with one-ton axles and 37–40" tires
- Military-wrap or full-wrap spring eyes are preferred for SOA applications — they resist unwrapping under braking loads

## Shock mount fabrication

### Lower shock mounts
- Weld-on tabs on the u-bolt plate are the most common approach
- Alternative: weld-on tabs directly on the axle tube
- Position to allow proper shock angle (slightly inward, bottom to top)

### Upper shock mounts
- Mount to the XJ frame rail
- Stock XJ upper rear shock mounts may be too low for the new geometry — relocate higher on the frame if needed
- Some builders run frame-mounted shock hoops or towers

### Shock selection
- Same considerations as the front: shocks must be valved for the increased unsprung weight
- Measure full droop-to-compression travel distance and select shocks that match
- Heavy-duty monotube or adjustable reservoir shocks recommended

## Bump stop placement

### Purpose
Prevent the Sterling housing from contacting the XJ floor pan, gas tank, or exhaust at full compression.

### Placement guidelines
- Mount bump stops to the XJ frame, positioned to contact the spring perch or axle tube at the desired maximum compression point
- Allow 1–1.5" clearance between the housing (at its highest point — typically the differential cover) and the nearest body/frame component at the bump stop contact point
- Progressive-rate bump stops reduce harshness

### Critical clearance check
At the bump stop contact point, verify:
- Differential cover to floor pan clearance
- Full-float hub to inner fender clearance
- Exhaust to housing clearance (relocate exhaust if necessary)
- Brake caliper to frame clearance

## Traction bars / anti-wrap

With one-ton axles and 37–40" tires, the torque transmitted through the rear leaf springs is significantly greater than stock. Under hard acceleration or engine braking:
- Leaf springs can flex into an S-shape (axle wrap)
- Severe wrap causes driveline vibration, leaf spring fatigue, and potential spring breakage

### Anti-wrap options

| Device | Description | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Traction bars (bolt-on or weld-on) | Bars that connect the axle to the frame, preventing the axle from rotating under torque | Most common solution. CalTracs, ladder bars, and custom fabricated options. |
| Anti-wrap leaf spring design | Stiffer, multi-leaf packs or springs with military wrap | Reduces but may not eliminate wrap. Works with traction bars. |
| Shackle orientation | Forward-facing shackles reduce wrap tendency vs rear-facing | Some XJ SOA builds use forward-facing shackles. Affects spring geometry. |

**Recommendation:** install traction bars on any one-ton rear axle build that will see hard off-road use or tow loads. The forces involved are well beyond what XJ leaf springs were designed to handle alone.

## U-bolt plate considerations

The u-bolt plate sits under the axle tube and clamps the spring pack to the axle. For one-ton builds:
- Use thick plate (3/8" minimum) — standard XJ thin plates will flex under the higher loads
- Incorporate lower shock mount tabs into the plate design when possible (reduces the number of weld-on brackets needed)
- The plate should be wide enough to contact the full width of the u-bolt span without edge-loading
